## 4. 30-DAY CONTENT STRATEGY

**Goal:** Establish visible presence as a credible, active cybersecurity professional. Not a student posting study notes — a practitioner sharing working knowledge.

**Posting cadence:** 3 posts per week (Monday, Wednesday, Friday). Each post takes 20–30 minutes maximum. No need for graphics — plain text LinkedIn posts outperform images for reach in professional content.

**Format principle:** Every post should open with a line that creates a reason to keep reading. No "Today I learned..." intros. Lead with the insight, not the journey.

---

### Week 1 — Establish your expertise baseline

**Post 1 (Monday) — The counterintuitive take**
Topic: "Why enterprise IT experience makes a better penetration tester"
Hook: "The best pentesters I know didn't start in security. They started in IT ops."
Content: 3–4 specific examples from your own experience — misconfigured AD, insecure EPOS integrations, endpoint gaps. What you saw as an IT admin that you now know an attacker would exploit immediately.
*Why this works:* Differentiates you. Most junior candidates talk about CTFs. You can talk about real enterprise gaps.

**Post 2 (Wednesday) — Tool breakdown**
Topic: How you use Burp Suite for web app testing (specific workflow, not a tutorial)
Hook: "Burp Suite has hundreds of features. Here are the 3 I use on every web app engagement."
Content: Intercept proxy workflow, Repeater for manual testing, Intruder for fuzzing. One concrete example.
*Why this works:* Practical content gets saved and reshared by practitioners.

**Post 3 (Friday) — Career positioning**
Topic: Your transition story — 200 words, honest and specific
Hook: "I spent 3 years keeping enterprise networks running. That's when I realised I wanted to break them instead."
Content: The moment that crystallised the decision. GCHQ MSc. CEH. What you're working toward.
*Why this works:* Human story + clear goal = memorable. Recruiters share this internally.

---

### Week 2 — Share working knowledge

**Post 4 (Monday) — OWASP Top 10 micro-series, post 1**
Topic: Injection vulnerabilities — what they look like in real enterprise apps
Hook: "SQL injection is 30 years old. It's still in the OWASP Top 10. Here's why."
Content: Brief explanation + one real-world context from your Eurostop security assessments (anonymised).

**Post 5 (Wednesday) — CTF writeup (brief)**
Topic: A TryHackMe or HackTheBox machine you recently completed
Hook: Name the machine. "Here's what the box taught me about [specific technique]."
Content: The attack chain in 5 steps. What you had to research that you didn't know before.
*Why this works:* Signals active practice, not passive credential collection.

**Post 6 (Friday) — Industry observation**
Topic: Comment on a UK cybersecurity news story (breach, new regulation, vulnerability disclosure)
Hook: State your take in the first sentence. Not "This is concerning" — something specific.
Content: 150–200 words with your actual analysis. Tag relevant companies or people if appropriate.
*Why this works:* Shows you follow the industry. Recruiters watch for this.

---

### Week 3 — Build credibility through depth

**Post 7 (Monday) — Dissertation spotlight**
Topic: Your ML Attacks on PUFs research — explained for a non-academic audience
Hook: "I used neural networks to impersonate a hardware chip. Here's how."
Content: What PUFs are, why they matter for hardware security, what GANs enabled. No jargon. End with: "This is why hardware-level security assumptions are worth questioning."
*Why this works:* Completely unique content. Nobody else at junior level is writing about this. Will attract senior practitioners.

**Post 8 (Wednesday) — Active Directory attack path**
Topic: The most common AD misconfigurations you see from an IT admin perspective
Hook: "I've configured Active Directory for enterprise clients. These are the mistakes that make pentesters happy."
Content: Kerberoasting exposure, default permissions, unconstrained delegation — explained without being a tutorial.

**Post 9 (Friday) — Certification reflection**
Topic: Honest CEH review — what it taught you and what it didn't
Hook: "The CEH gets criticised a lot. Some of it is fair. Here's my honest take."
Content: Concrete skills you gained, what you needed CTF labs to supplement, why you're pursuing eJPT next.
*Why this works:* Authentic content outperforms promotional content. Shows self-awareness.

---

### Week 4 — Reach and networking content

**Post 10 (Monday) — OSINT walkthrough**
Topic: Passive reconnaissance methodology — what you do before touching a target
Hook: "Before I run a single tool, I spend an hour doing this."
Content: OSINT workflow: Shodan, WHOIS, LinkedIn, job postings as attack surface intel, certificate transparency logs.

**Post 11 (Wednesday) — Advice for others in your position**
Topic: How to transition from IT support into penetration testing
Hook: "If you're in IT support and want to move into pentesting, here's what actually moved the needle for me."
Content: Specific, honest. TryHackMe path, CEH study, what to avoid. This will be shared widely.
*Why this works:* Advice content gets the highest shares on LinkedIn. Puts you in front of people you have not yet connected with.

**Post 12 (Friday) — Open for opportunities post**
Topic: Direct, professional availability signal
Hook: "I am actively looking for Junior Penetration Tester roles in the UK. Here is what I bring."
Content: Three specific skills with evidence. Portfolio link. Resume link. Direct ask for referrals or introductions.
*Why this works:* Done well, these posts work. The key is specificity — not "looking for opportunities" but "looking for X, with Y, in Z."

---

**Content rules to follow throughout:**

1. Never post without a hook in the first line. The first 2 lines determine whether anyone reads the rest.
2. Keep posts between 150–300 words. LinkedIn compresses longer posts.
3. End every technical post with a question or a direct observation that invites comment.
4. Reply to every comment within 24 hours. LinkedIn's algorithm rewards comment velocity.
5. Do not cross-post from Twitter/X. Write for LinkedIn specifically.
6. Tag sparingly and only when relevant — no more than 2–3 tags per post.

## 5. CONNECTION STRATEGY

### Who to connect with (in priority order)

**Tier 1 — Direct hiring influence (connect first)**
- Recruitment consultants and headhunters specialising in cybersecurity in the UK (search: "cybersecurity recruiter" + "penetration testing" + London/UK)
- Hiring managers with titles: Head of Security, CISO, Security Engineering Lead at companies on your target list
- Directors and partners at specialist security consultancies: NCC Group, Pen Test Partners, Context IS, Prism Infosec, Secureworks UK

**Tier 2 — Community and credibility (connect second)**
- Other penetration testers with 1–4 years experience (your peer group — they share job leads, refer each other)
- CTF community members active on LinkedIn (TryHackMe and HackTheBox have LinkedIn communities)
- MSc alumni from Queen's University Belfast Computer Science / Cyber Security programmes
- NCSC community members and CiSP (Cyber Security Information Sharing Partnership) participants

**Tier 3 — Long-term relationship building**
- Red team practitioners with 5+ years experience (follow, engage with their content, connect after 2–3 meaningful interactions)
- Academic connections from QUB — professors, former coursemates now in industry
- UK cybersecurity journalists and conference organisers (BSides, Infosecurity Europe, CyberUK)

---

### How many to connect with

Send 10–15 connection requests per day. LinkedIn limits and quality both matter. Do not send bulk generic requests — personalise every single one.

---

LinkedIn limits connection notes to 300 characters. Below are templates for each tier. Customise the [bracketed] parts.

```
Hi [Name], I'm a CEH-certified junior pentester with an MSc from QUB (GCHQ-accredited), actively seeking roles in the UK. Your work at [Agency] looks relevant — happy to connect and stay on your radar. Thanks.
```

**For security professionals at target companies:**
```

```

**For peers (other junior pentesters):**
```
Hi [Name], I noticed you're also working through [TryHackMe/HTB/CEH journey]. Would be great to connect — always useful to know others navigating the same path into offensive security.
```

**For red team senior practitioners (after engaging with their content):**
```
Hi [Name], I've followed your posts on [topic] closely — [specific insight from their post]. I'm a junior transitioning into pentesting and your perspective is one I find genuinely useful. Would be glad to connect.
```

---

### Engagement rules (equally important as connection count)

- Comment on posts from target company employees and senior practitioners before connecting. Meaningful comments (2–3 sentences, adds something) make your name recognisable before the request lands.
- Like strategically — likes are invisible to your feed but visible to the poster. Consistent engagement with a target person's content builds familiarity.
- Do not comment "Great post!" — it wastes the interaction. Say something specific or say nothing.
- Post on your own profile regularly (per the content strategy above) so that when a recruiter visits your profile after seeing your comment, they see activity and credibility.
